Problem Background

Viktor Lenac Shipyard, founded in 1896 in Rijeka, Croatia, built its reputation on ship repairs. A planned $80M modernization program financed by the International Finance Corporation expanded product offerings into conversions, offshore jackets, and newbuilding. However, delays and cost overruns left the yard without the necessary infrastructure, causing fixed costs to surge beyond what the contract portfolio could support — ultimately pushing Viktor Lenac into bankruptcy.


💡 Core Question: Can changing the contract mix maximize contribution and close the $13.4M gap — without additional capital expenditure?

Why Integer Linear Programming?
01
Linear Objective
Each contract contributes a fixed dollar amount — total contribution is a linear combination of contract quantities.
02
Linear Constraints
All resource usage scales proportionally with the number of contracts accepted — satisfying LP linearity requirements.
03
Integer Requirement
Contracts must be whole numbers — you cannot accept half a repair or a fraction of a conversion. ILP ensures practical solutions.

Why This Mix?
Why max Conversions & Offshore Jackets?
Highest return per unit of resource consumed — $5,014,710 and $3,098,844 per contract respectively.
Why only 1 Repair?
After 4 Conversions + 2 Offshore Jackets consume 5,236 tons of steel, only 264 tons remain — enough for exactly one Repair (170 tons). A second Repair requires 340 tons.
Why 0 Newbuildings?
Newbuilding requires 300 tons of steel — but only 264 tons remain after higher-value contracts. Lowest contribution per resource unit.

The $3.2M Gap — Why It Matters
Bankruptcy Exposure
Viktor Lenac operates under bankruptcy protection, sustained by ongoing cash infusions. Below-$30M contribution means the yard continues to consume cash rather than generate it.
Creditor Risk
IFC and private lenders behind the $80M modernization loan face continued non-performance — risking accelerated debt-service demands or forced asset sales.
Modernization Blockage
Covering fixed costs is a prerequisite for retaining skilled workers and funding Phase 2 modernization — both essential for long-term competitiveness.
ℹ Recommendations are ranked by priority and potential impact. Actions 1–2 are immediate; Actions 3–5 are structural and medium-term.
1
★ HIGHEST PRIORITY
Secure Additional Steel Supply
Only 76 additional tons needed to unlock a 2nd Repair contract. At the shadow price of $4,558.83/ton, the max premium is ~$345K — well below the $520K contribution it unlocks. Negotiate with Czech Republic supplier or explore equivalent-quality alternatives. Larger increases (up to 336 tons) could unlock further contracts.
2
HIGH PRIORITY
Pursue More High-Value Contracts
Currently only 4 Conversions and 2 Offshore Jackets are available — capping the model's earning potential. Management should focus business development on attracting more high-value clients. Expanding this pipeline is the fastest route to raising the contribution ceiling.
3
Address Fixed Costs Alongside Revenue
Even the optimal plan falls $3.2M short. The gap is a cost structure problem as much as a revenue problem. Management should renegotiate financial obligations (including IFC debt), improve operational efficiency, and defer non-essential investments until revenue stabilizes.
4
Deprioritize Newbuilding Short-Term
Newbuilding delivers the lowest contribution per resource unit and cannot fit in the optimal plan under current steel constraints. While important for long-term growth, it should be deprioritized until the financial position stabilizes and constraints are relieved.
5
Accelerate Phase 2 Modernization
Phase 2 modernization is the root cause of the current crisis. Completing the infrastructure reduces per-contract resource usage, expands capacity for high-value work, and is a prerequisite for retaining skilled workers. This is essential for long-term competitiveness and closing the remaining gap between current performance and financial sustainability.

The Bottom Line

Viktor Lenac Shipyard's current production plan generates approximately $16.6M in contribution — well short of the $30M breakeven target. This Integer Linear Programming analysis demonstrates that an optimized contract mix can improve contribution by 61% to $26.8M, representing a significant and immediately achievable gain.

However, the analysis also confirms that production planning optimization alone is not sufficient to close the remaining $3.2M gap. Steel emerged as the sole binding constraint — the only resource limiting Viktor Lenac's earning potential under current conditions.

✓ Management should adopt the optimized production plan immediately to capture the available $10.2M improvement, while simultaneously pursuing fixed cost reduction, steel supply expansion, high-value contract development, and Phase 2 modernization acceleration.
"With the right combination of short-term optimization and long-term structural reform, Viktor Lenac has a viable path toward financial stability and long-term competitiveness."
